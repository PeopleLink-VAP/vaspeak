# GCSE English Language Exam Techniques (2026)

## Command Words — What They Mean and What to Do

Always underline the command word before starting. It tells you exactly what skill is being tested.

| Command Word | What to do | Common Mistake |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|
| **List / Identify / Give** | Short factual answers — one or two words or phrases retrieved directly from the text | Writing full paragraphs wastes time and marks |
| **Summarise** | Pick out the key ideas on a theme from one or both texts and put them in your own words | Copying chunks of text verbatim — must be paraphrased |
| **How does the writer use language** | Analyse the techniques used and explain the specific effects they have on the reader | Listing devices without explaining *why* — this is "feature-spotting" |
| **How does the writer structure** | Analyse paragraph and sentence-level choices, shifts in focus, and development from start to end | Only commenting on language rather than structure |
| **Compare** | Look at both texts: similarities AND differences in perspectives and methods; use comparative language | Writing about one text, then the other — must be woven together |
| **Evaluate / To what extent** | Give a critical judgement about the text in response to a statement; justify it with textual evidence | Agreeing with the statement throughout — top marks require weighing both sides |

## Assessment Objectives: What Examiners Are Marking

| AO | What It Tests | How to Show It |
|----|--------------|---------------|
| **AO1** | Select and interpret evidence — both obvious and implied meaning | Choose precise, relevant quotes; read "between the lines" for implicit meaning |
| **AO2** | Analyse writer's language and structure, using subject terminology | Name the technique, quote the evidence, explain the effect on the reader |
| **AO3** | Compare writers' perspectives and methods across two texts | Integrated comparison using "whereas", "similarly", "in contrast" |
| **AO4** | Evaluate texts critically in response to a statement | Form a clear judgement; agree and disagree with evidence for both |
| **AO5** | Communicate effectively; organise ideas clearly | Plan before writing; use paragraphs and discourse markers |
| **AO6** | Technical accuracy: vocabulary, sentence structures, SPaG | Proof-read; vary sentence structures; avoid comma splicing |

## The PEAL / PETAL Method for Analysis Paragraphs

Use these frameworks for structuring paragraphs in **reading analysis questions** (Q2, Q3, Q4 on Paper 1; Q3, Q4 on Paper 2):

### PEAL (starter scaffold)
- **P** — **Point:** A clear topic sentence that directly answers the question
- **E** — **Evidence:** A short, embedded quotation from the text
- **A** — **Analysis:** Explain *how* the technique works and *why* the writer used it
- **L** — **Link:** Connect back to the question or the writer's overall intention

### PETAL (for higher marks — add the technique)
- **P** — Point
- **E** — Evidence
- **T** — **Technique:** Name the device (e.g. metaphor, sibilance, short sentence)
- **A** — Analysis of the effect
- **L** — Link

### PETER (alternative — adds reader response)
- **P** — Point / **E** — Evidence / **T** — Technique / **E** — Explain the effect / **R** — **Reader Response:** "This makes the reader feel..."

> **Important:** These are scaffolds, not straitjackets. At Grade 8/9 level, move beyond a rigid formula. Embed short quotations fluidly in sentences and develop a perceptive, exploratory analysis rather than repeating the same pattern. Examiners warn against "over-reliance on simple PEE-type responses" for higher-attaining students.

## Language Terminology — What to Name and Use

### Figurative Language
| Device | Definition | Example effect |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Metaphor** | Describing something as if it *is* something else | Creates a direct, vivid image; implies the comparison is absolute |
| **Simile** | Comparing using "like" or "as" | Similarly vivid but draws attention to the act of comparing |
| **Personification** | Giving human qualities to non-human things | Makes abstract or natural things feel threatening/welcoming |
| **Pathetic fallacy** | Using weather/nature to reflect mood or emotion | Creates atmosphere that mirrors a character's state of mind |
| **Hyperbole** | Deliberate extreme exaggeration | Emphasises intensity; can create humour or urgency |
| **Oxymoron** | Two contradictory terms placed together | Creates tension or highlights a paradox |
| **Symbolism** | An object/image representing a larger idea | Adds layers of meaning beneath the surface |

### Sound Devices
| Device | Definition |
|--------|-----------|
| **Alliteration** | Repetition of the same consonant sound at the start of nearby words |
| **Sibilance** | Repetition of "s" or "sh" sounds — creates a hissing or whispering effect |
| **Onomatopoeia** | Words whose sounds imitate the thing they describe (e.g. "crash", "whisper") |
| **Assonance** | Repetition of vowel sounds within words |

### Vocabulary and Word Choice
- **Connotations:** The associations of a word beyond its literal meaning (e.g. "home" = warmth, safety, belonging)
- **Semantic field:** A group of words sharing a common theme (e.g. military language used to describe a business)
- **Register:** The level of formality in language choices (formal/informal, technical/colloquial)
- **Tone:** The writer's attitude as conveyed through language (e.g. angry, nostalgic, ironic, foreboding)

### Structural Terminology
| Device | What to comment on |
|--------|--------------------|
| **Sentence length** | Short sentences — tension, shock, climax. Long, complex sentences — building detail, accumulating pressure |
| **Paragraph length** | A single-sentence paragraph creates emphasis and stands out visually |
| **Cyclical structure** | The ending echoes the opening — creates a sense of resolution or irony |
| **Linear/chronological structure** | Events told in order — creates a building sense of time passing |
| **Flashback** | Disrupts chronology — contrasts past and present, creates nostalgia or regret |
| **Contrast / Juxtaposition** | Placing opposites together — highlights the difference, creates tension |
| **Shift in focus or perspective** | Moving from broad to narrow (or vice versa) — zooming in/out on a subject |
| **Repetition** | Reinforces an idea; can be anaphora (repetition at the start of sentences) |

## Analysing Structure (the Harder Skill)

Structure analysis (Paper 1 Q3) is the question many students find hardest. Follow this approach:

1. **Track from start to end:** What is the text doing at the *beginning*? What changes in the *middle*? How does it *end*? What has shifted — focus, mood, perspective, pace?
2. **Comment on sentence and paragraph level:** Vary between commenting on whole-text structure and sentence-level choices.
3. **Comment on the effect on the reader:** Not just *what* the structural feature is, but *how* it makes the reader feel or react.

### Useful Structural Observations for Fiction
- Starts with action/dialogue/setting — creates immediate immersion vs. starts with background — builds context
- Shifts from a third-person narrator to a close, internal first-person perspective — increases intimacy
- Ends on an unresolved note or question — creates ambiguity, encourages reflection
- The writer withholds key information until the end — builds suspense

## Answering the Evaluation Question (Paper 1 Q4 — 20 marks)

This is the highest-tariff reading question. A statement about the text is given; you must evaluate how successfully the writer does what the statement claims.

### Strategy
1. **Form a clear view first** — do you mostly agree or mostly disagree? Both are valid.
2. **Use evidence to agree** — find 2–3 moments in the text that support the statement; analyse how the writing achieves this.
3. **Use evidence to challenge** — find at least one moment that complicates or contradicts the statement.
4. **Maintain critical distance** — avoid just agreeing throughout; examiners reward nuanced, balanced judgement.
5. **Refer back to the statement regularly** — keep the question in view throughout.

## Answering the Comparison Question (Paper 2 Q4 — 16 marks)

This tests AO3 — comparing writers' perspectives *and* methods.

### Integrated Comparison (not "text dump" comparison)
- **Wrong approach:** Write everything about Text A, then everything about Text B.
- **Right approach:** Make a point about both texts in the same paragraph using comparative language.

### Comparative Discourse Markers
- Similarity: *Similarly, both writers... / In the same way... / Like [writer A], [writer B]...*
- Difference: *In contrast, [writer B]... / Whereas [writer A]..., [writer B]... / However...*
- Nuance: *While both writers..., [writer B] differs in... / Although [writer A]..., [writer B] conveys a more...*

### What to Compare
- The **perspective** each writer takes (personal/impersonal, positive/critical, emotional/detached)
- The **language techniques** each uses and their effects
- The **tone** and how it shifts within each text
- The **audience** each writer is addressing

## DAFOREST — Persuasive Techniques for Paper 2 Writing

When writing a speech, article, or letter (Paper 2 Section B), deploy these persuasive techniques:

| Letter | Technique | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **D** | Direct Address | "You know how important this is." |
| **A** | Alliteration / Anecdote | "Every expert agrees..." / Open with a personal story |
| **F** | Facts | "Research shows that 1 in 3 students..." |
| **O** | Opinion / Opposing view (then counter it) | "Some argue... however, this ignores..." |
| **R** | Repetition / Rhetorical Questions | "Do we really want this? Can we afford to wait?" |
| **E** | Emotive Language / Exaggeration | "devastating consequences", "thousands of lives at stake" |
| **S** | Statistics | "Over 70% of young people report..." |
| **T** | Triplets (Rule of Three) | "It is unfair, it is unnecessary, and it must stop." |

## Words That Limit Your Marks

Avoid vague or imprecise language that earns no credit:

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|------------|
| **"The writer creates tension"** (unsupported) | Follow with *how* and *why*: which word/technique creates it? |
| **"This is interesting"** | Say *specifically* what is interesting and *why* it has an effect |
| **"The writer uses good vocabulary"** | Name the specific words and explain their connotations |
| **"It makes you feel sad/scared"** | Be precise: "...creates a sense of foreboding by..." |
| **"The word 'X' is effective"** | Explain the connotations: "The word 'X' implies..." |

## Time Management in the Exam

AQA: Both papers are 1h 45min for 80 marks — approximately **1 mark per minute**, with 5 minutes for planning/checking.

### Suggested AQA Time Allocation

**Paper 1:**
- Q1 (4 marks): 3–4 minutes — **multiple choice** in 2026; faster than the old "list four things" format
- Q2 (8 marks): 10 minutes
- Q3 (8 marks): 10 minutes — answer must focus on the **specific effect named** in the question (e.g. mystery, tension)
- Q4 (20 marks): 25 minutes
- Section B writing (40 marks): 45 minutes (including 5–10 min planning)
- Buffer: 5 minutes to check

**Paper 2:**
- Q1 (4 marks): 4–5 minutes
- Q2 (8 marks): 10 minutes
- Q3 (12 marks): 15 minutes
- Q4 (16 marks): 20 minutes
- Section B writing (40 marks): 45 minutes (including 5–10 min planning)
- Buffer: 5 minutes to check

> **The most common reason students drop marks: spending too long on lower-tariff questions and running out of time for the 40-mark writing section.**
