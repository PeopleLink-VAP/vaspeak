# GCSE English Language Writing Guide (2026)

Both Paper 1 and Paper 2 contain a **40-mark writing task**. This is split 24 marks (AO5: content and organisation) + 16 marks (AO6: technical accuracy). Strong writing can transform a grade.

---

## Paper 1 — Creative Writing (Descriptive or Narrative)

### What Examiners Want to See

Examiners reward writing that:
- Shows **authorial control** — deliberate, purposeful choices, not just a sequence of events
- Creates **atmosphere and emotion** — the reader should *feel* something
- Uses **varied and precise vocabulary** — powerful verbs and specific nouns over vague adjectives
- Demonstrates **technical control** — correct punctuation, consistent tense, varied sentence structures

### The Key Secret: Don't Tell a Full Plot

Under timed exam conditions, students who try to tell a complete story often rush the ending, lose tense control, and produce underdeveloped writing. Instead:

> **Focus on a specific moment, opening, or scene** — write it in high detail with atmosphere, sensory language, and emotional depth.

This allows you to:
- Develop one moment slowly and deliberately
- Show foreshadowing, symbolism, and motifs
- Craft a satisfying cyclical structure (ending echoes the opening)

---

### Techniques for High-Band Creative Writing

#### Openings
Start with something that immediately creates atmosphere or intrigue:
- **In medias res** — drop the reader into the middle of action or tension
- **A striking image or sound** — "The door had not moved for three days."
- **A question or statement that disrupts expectation** — "The day my father came home was the worst day of my life."

Avoid openings that begin: "It was a sunny day..." or "Once upon a time..." — these are generic.

#### Sensory Detail
Use all five senses — not just visual. Uncommon senses (smell, touch, taste) make writing stand out:
- Sight: "the milk-white sky pressing down on the rooftops"
- Sound: "the creak of the third stair, familiar as a bad dream"
- Smell: "damp concrete and diesel fumes — the smell of the town she'd never managed to leave"
- Touch: "the banister, sticky and warm under her palm"
- Taste: "a metallic taste at the back of her throat — fear, she knew, without naming it"

#### Figurative Language for Atmosphere
- Pathetic fallacy: weather that mirrors mood — "the rain arrived like a verdict"
- Extended metaphor woven throughout a piece — more sophisticated than one-off similes
- Motifs: a recurring image (colour, object, sound) that gains meaning each time it appears
- Personification of setting: "the house watched her come and go"

#### Sentence Variation for Effect
- **Short sentence for impact:** "She didn't look back."
- **Long, complex sentence for accumulation:** "The corridor stretched ahead of her, impossibly long, its walls pressing in with each step, the air growing thick with something she couldn't name but recognised all the same."
- **Single-sentence paragraph:** Reserve for the most important moments — it creates instant emphasis.
- **Grammatical patterning:** Starting several consecutive sentences the same way builds rhythm and intensity: "She could hear it. She could smell it. She could feel it on her skin."

#### Structural Devices
- **Cyclical structure:** Echo the opening in the ending — with a subtle shift in meaning or emotional register.
- **Withholding information:** Don't reveal a key detail until the writer chooses — builds curiosity and tension.
- **Flashback:** A single memory embedded in the present narrative can deepen character and create contrast.
- **Shifting narrative distance:** Move from broad, distant observation (third person) to close, internal thought — creates intimacy at critical moments.

### Technical Accuracy for Creative Writing (AO6)

**Common errors that lose marks:**

| Error | What it is | Fix |
|-------|-----------|-----|
| **Comma splice** | Joining two complete sentences with only a comma | Use a full stop, semicolon, or a conjunction |
| **Tense inconsistency** | Slipping between past and present tense mid-piece | Choose one tense and stick to it; simple past is safest |
| **No paragraphing** | Walls of text with no breaks | New paragraph for: new focus, time shift, new character action, new idea |
| **Apostrophe misuse** | "it's" vs "its", "your" vs "you're" | Possessive "its" has no apostrophe; "it's" = "it is" |
| **Missing full stops** | Run-on sentences that don't end | Every sentence must end |
| **Overuse of adverbs** | "She walked slowly and carefully and silently" | Replace with one powerful verb: "She crept" |

**A simple tense rule:** Most examiners find **simple past tense** the safest and most versatile choice for narrative writing. Present tense can work for atmosphere but is harder to sustain under pressure.

## Paper 2 — Transactional / Persuasive Writing

Paper 2 Section B tests **AO5** (communicating a viewpoint effectively for a specific audience and purpose) and **AO6** (technical accuracy).

### Common Task Formats
- **Speech** — to be delivered to a specified audience (e.g. school assembly, local council)
- **Newspaper or magazine article** — including a headline and possibly a subheading
- **Letter** — formal or informal, to a named recipient
- **Leaflet** — informative and persuasive, often with subheadings
- **Blog post / online article** — informal register, direct address, subheadings

### Adapting Register for the Task

| Format | Register | Features to include |
|--------|----------|-------------------|
| Formal speech | Formal but passionate | Direct address, rhetorical questions, DAFOREST devices |
| Newspaper article | Formal, authoritative | Headline, subheading, third-person reporting, quotes/statistics |
| Letter to authority | Formal, persuasive | Proper salutation/sign-off, structured argument |
| Blog / teen magazine article | Informal, conversational | Chatty tone, second person ("you"), short paragraphs, subheadings |

### Structuring a Persuasive Piece

1. **Opening:** Engage the reader immediately — a shocking fact, a rhetorical question, or an anecdote
2. **Point 1:** Strongest argument — evidence/example → explain the significance
3. **Point 2:** Second argument — broadens the case
4. **Counter-argument:** Acknowledge the opposing view then dismiss it ("While some claim..., this argument fails to account for...")
5. **Conclusion:** Return to the opening theme; end with a call to action or memorable final line

### Rhetorical Devices (DAFOREST — see exam-techniques.md)
Layer at least 4–5 different devices throughout. Don't use them all in the opening paragraph — spread them through the piece so the writing feels varied, not mechanical.

### Discourse Markers for Persuasive Writing
- **To add:** Furthermore, In addition, Moreover, Not only... but also
- **To contrast:** However, Nevertheless, Despite this, On the other hand
- **To concede then rebut:** Admittedly..., yet... / While it is true that..., this overlooks...
- **To conclude:** Ultimately, In conclusion, It is clear that

---

### Technical Accuracy for Transactional Writing (AO6)

The same accuracy rules apply as in creative writing, with additional considerations:

- **Consistent formal register:** Don't slip into slang if the task is formal
- **Appropriate format features:** Include a headline for an article; "Dear..." and "Yours sincerely/faithfully" for a formal letter
- **Varied sentence structures:** Mix simple, compound, and complex sentences deliberately
- **Precise vocabulary:** Use subject-specific words (for a persuasive speech about the environment, use "biodiversity", "carbon footprint", "ecosystem")
- **Paragraph cohesion:** Each paragraph should develop one point; use topic sentences to open each paragraph

## Planning — A Non-Negotiable Step

Examiners consistently report that students who spend **5–10 minutes planning** outperform those who dive straight in.

### A Quick Planning Method (5 minutes)
1. Read the task carefully — identify the audience, purpose, and format
2. Jot down 4–5 key ideas or points (bullet points only)
3. Decide the order — which point is strongest? (save it for end, or open with it)
4. Note 2–3 vocabulary choices or techniques you want to use
5. Decide the first line — write something that hooks immediately

For creative writing, also sketch:
- The opening moment
- The emotional journey (what changes?)
- The final image or line

---

## Proofreading — Reserve 3–5 Minutes

Read your answer as if you're the teacher marking it. Look specifically for:
- Tense changes (especially in narrative writing)
- Missing full stops or capital letters
- Comma splices (two sentences joined by only a comma)
- Apostrophe errors
- Repeated words — can any be replaced with a more precise synonym?

A polished, carefully proofread piece at Grade 6 will often outscore a more ambitious but careless piece at Grade 8.

## Grade Descriptors at a Glance

| Grade | AO5 Writing Quality | AO6 Accuracy |
|-------|--------------------|----|
| 9 | Compelling, sophisticated, deliberate choices, highly manipulates reader response | Extensive and varied vocabulary; varied and inventive sentence structures; virtually no errors |
| 7–8 | Crafted, controlled, purposeful techniques, clear authorial voice | Wide, sophisticated vocabulary; varied structures; few errors |
| 5–6 | Clear and consistent; some deliberate language choices; generally organised | Increasingly varied vocabulary; some varied structures; some errors |
| 3–4 | Mostly communicates intent; some structure; limited technique | Simple vocabulary; simple sentence structures; frequent errors |
| 1–2 | Simple, limited, partially relevant | Limited vocabulary; mostly simple sentences; many errors |
